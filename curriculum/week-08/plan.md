## Interview Prep Questions and Model Answers
### Q: Explain stationarity in plain English.
A: Stationarity should first be explained as an intuitive idea before any notation. In quant work it matters because it shapes how we measure, compare, or model financial behavior inside time series i: stationarity, ar/ma/arima intuition, walk-forward testing, and forecasting tasks.

### Q: What is a common mistake when applying autocorrelation in finance?
A: A common mistake is to use autocorrelation mechanically while ignoring assumptions, data quality, or market frictions. In finance, even a mathematically correct method can fail if the data are noisy, the sample is unstable, or the implementation is unrealistic.

### Q: How would you test whether your work on time series i: stationarity, ar/ma/arima intuition, walk-forward testing, and forecasting is robust?
A: I would start with a simple baseline, check sensitivity to assumptions, inspect subperiod stability, and verify that results survive realistic costs or constraints. If the topic involves prediction or backtesting, I would also guard against leakage and ensure evaluation is aligned with how the model would be used live.

### Q: Why does ar/ma/arima matter for a quant trader or quant researcher?
A: AR/MA/ARIMA matters because it changes either signal quality, risk control, implementation quality, or communication with a desk or portfolio manager. A useful quant answer should always connect the concept to a real decision rather than leaving it as theory only.


## Capstone
- Problem statement: Forecast a simple financial time series baseline and compare it against naive rules.
- Data source suggestions: Use a daily ETF series or FX pair from yfinance; for safer modeling, focus on rolling volatility or spread changes instead of raw prices.
- Implementation steps: Clean the data, check stationarity visually, define a target, split with walk-forward validation, fit naive and time-series baselines, and compare errors.
- Evaluation metrics: MAE, RMSE, directional accuracy if relevant, and economic interpretation of forecast usefulness.
- Common mistakes: Forecasting price levels without understanding non-stationarity, leaking future data into features, or celebrating tiny statistical gains with no economic meaning.
